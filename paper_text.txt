CoLLM: Industrial Large–Small Model
Collaboration With Fuzzy Decision-Making
Agent and Self-Reﬂection
Haiteng Wang
, Member, IEEE, Lei Ren
, Senior Member, IEEE, Tuo Zhao

Abstract—In industrial applications, large models have exhib-
ited superior generalization capabilities that are unattainable with
smaller models. However, when faced with edge scenarios and
highly diverse industrial samples, their deployment remains chal-
lenging due to high computational costs and unreliable output. To
address these challenges, we propose a large–small model collab-
orative framework (CoLLM), which dynamically selects between
small and large models based on the characteristics exhibited by the
samples. Speciﬁcally, this approach estimates uncertainty from in-
put samples to guide model selection: low-uncertainty samples are
processed by the small model for efﬁciency, while high-uncertainty
or complex samples are routed to the large model for im-
proved accuracy. It ﬁrst constructs a fuzzy decision-making agent
based on the fuzzy neural network to assess sample complexity
and determine the appropriate model for inference. Furthermore,
a self-reﬂection mechanism is proposed to reﬁne the large model’s
output, reducing the risk of unreliable output. Experimental results
in industrial time-series datasets demonstrate that our framework
improves the computational efﬁciency of large models up to 14.54×
while maintaining or improving prediction accuracy.
Index Terms—Foundation model, industrial large model, large
language model (LLM), industrial time series, mixture of experts
(MoE).

Recently, researchers have explored collaborative inference

provide superior generalization and the capability to handle

LM usage by 50% while maintaining response quality. However,
to develop a fast and reliable large–small model collaborative
inference framework, the following technology issues need to
be addressed.

II. RELATED WORK
A. LMs for Time-Series Analysis
Current time-series modeling approaches are mainly clas-
siﬁed into two categories: pretrained large time-series mod-
els [20], [21] and ﬁne-tuning LLMs [22], [23], [24], [34]
for time-series tasks. Pretrained large time-series models en-
compass Transformer-based models, contrastive learning-based
models, convolutional network-based models, decomposition-
based models, graph neural network-based models, and gen-
erative models. For example, the Transformer-based model
Moirai [20] addresses heterogeneity in arbitrary time-series data
by leveraging attention mechanisms for different variables and
mixed parametric distributions. Similarly, the Timer model [21]
uniﬁes heterogeneous time-series data into a single-sequence
structure (S3) and reformulates time-series forecasting, imputa-
tion, and anomaly detection as a uniﬁed generative task, thereby
mitigating data heterogeneity issues. However, these models
require extensive time-series data for effective performance.
Another alternative strategy is to ﬁne-tune LLMs for
time-series tasks. For instance, TIME-LLM [22] ﬁne-tunes
LLMs using patch reprogramming and employs patch as
prompt to address modality heterogeneity, enhancing the
model’s ability to understand time-series data. The aLLM4TS

This stage ﬁlters simple samples via initial predictions from the
SM, reducing the frequency of LM invocation.

D. LM Architecture and Training Optimization
1) LM Architecture: The proposed LM is based on the pre-
trained GPT-2 architecture, employing a frozen parameter strat-
egy that retains the self-attention mechanism and feed-forward
neural network modules learned during pretraining. These com-
ponents capture rich language features and are leveraged for
time-series processing. To accommodate time-series data, the
input layer is redesigned with a patch embedding mechanism.
Speciﬁcally, time-series data are segmented into multiple local
patches, each mapped to high-dimensional vectors via a linear
transformation, forming input embeddings.
2) Multistage Training Strategy: A three-stage progressive
training strategy is proposed to achieve modular cooperative
inference and parameter decoupling optimization, employing a
hierarchical parameter freezing mechanism to reduce gradient
interference risks.
Training Stage 1: The SM (S) is ﬁrst trained independently,
with all other modules frozen. End-to-end optimization min-
imizes prediction errors, enabling S to establish time-series
feature representations.
Training Stage 2: The LM (L) is ﬁne-tuned with S’s
parameters frozen to prevent optimization interference from S’s
backpropagation path.
Training Stage 3: In the ﬁnal stage, the self-reﬂection model
(R) and decision agent (F) are trained, with all other module
parameters frozen. R and F learn the joint distribution of input
features and predictions via neural networks, constructing a
conﬁdence-driven dynamic routing mechanism.

A. Experimental Setup
1) Dataset: In this section, we will introduce the aero-engine
datasets used in the article. The Commercial Modular Aero-
Propulsion System Simulation (CMAPSS) dataset [25], released
by the National Aeronautics and Space Administration in 2008,
has become a widely used benchmark in the ﬁeld of remaining
useful life (RUL) prediction. This dataset provides a detailed
record of the complete degradation process of turbofan engines
from normal operation to failure, offering researchers a wealth
of realistic degradation data.
Within the CMAPSS dataset, the operational status of the
turbofan engine is monitored by 21 different sensors, measuring
parameters, such as time, pressure, and rotational speed. How-
ever, since the readings of sensors 1, 5, 6, 10, 16, 18, and 19
remain constant throughout the process, they show little cor-
relation with changes in RUL. Consequently, these sensors are
removed, and the remaining 14 sensors are used for prediction
tasks. In addition, the CMAPSS dataset is divided into multiple
subsets to accommodate testing under different scenarios and
operating conditions. In this study, we selected two of these
subsets, FD001 and FD003, to evaluate the performance of our
model.

complexity of the model. FLOPs are independent of the run-
time environment [whether CPU, GPU, or tensor processing
unit (TPU)], reﬂecting only the model’s inherent computational
demands. Generally, the larger the FLOPs, the higher the compu-
tational overhead. Therefore, under the same level of accuracy,
a model with fewer FLOPs is more efﬁcient and better suited for
industrial applications.
B. Main Results
We conducted experiments on the FD001 and FD003 datasets
using LMs, such as GPT-2, Llama2-7b, One Fits All, Autotimes,
Qwen2.5-0.5b, and Qwen3-0.6b. To ensure fairness, the subnet-
works in the dynamic architectures employ the same parameter
conﬁgurations as those of their base networks. Models with
1-Epoch training refer to training for a single epoch, primar-
ily to enable the language model to acquire basic capability
in handling time-series data, while ﬁne-tuning indicates thor-
ough ﬁne-tuning with multiple epochs. We then implemented
CoLLM by leveraging the ﬁne-tuned Transformer encoder as
the SM and One Fits All as the LM. We varied the conﬁdence
thresholds and evaluated the corresponding performance and
speed of CoLLM. A higher threshold τ1 means that fewer
samples exit at the SM, thus requiring more computational
resources for the LM’s prediction. τ2 represents the threshold for
LLMs to engage in self-reﬂection. A higher value of τ2 corre-
sponds to fewer samples being subjected to the model’s self-
reﬂection mechanism. For the FD001 dataset, the threshold
settings are as follows: 1) CoLLM-A [0.3, 0.1], 2) CoLLM-B
[0.4, 0.1], and 3) CoLLM-C [0.6, 0.05]. For the FD003 dataset,
the thresholds are conﬁgured as: 1) CoLLM-A [0.15, 0.1], 2)
CoLLM-B [0.4, 0.1], and 3) CoLLM-C [0.6, 0.05]. The speedup
is measured by calculating FLOPs and comparing it with that of
the baseline LM. The experimental results are given in Table II.
On the FD001 dataset, CoLLM-A achieves an RMSE of
12.45 and an MAE of 9.13, with accuracies of 99.1% and
97.3%, respectively, while reducing FLOPs by a factor of 3.88.
CoLLM-CachievesanRMSEof12.33andanMAEof8.86,with
accuracies of 100.1% and 100.3%, respectively, while reducing
FLOPs by a factor of 1.26. On the FD003 dataset, CoLLM-A
achieves an RMSE of 11.26 and an MAE of 7.42, with accuracies
of 99.3% and 95.2%, respectively, while reducing FLOPs by a
factor of 14.54. CoLLM-C achieves an RMSE of 11.11 and
an MAE of 7.12, with accuracies of 101.44% and 100.42%
respectively, while reducing FLOPs by a factor of 1.57. These
experimental results show that, despite performance and dataset
differences,CoLLMcanacceleratecomputations(upto14.54×)
while sustaining minimal accuracy loss or even achieving better
performance. Fig. 3 presents examples of several prediction

the ground truth. From the lower part of Fig. 3, one can observe
that the prediction error aligns well with the actual error, demon-

C. Effect of the Self-Reﬂection
To assess the effectiveness of the self-reﬂection mechanism,
we conducted an ablation study. We tested the CoLLM on
the FD003 dataset and adjusted various conﬁdence thresholds
for the submodels. The term “number of sample reﬂections”
refers to the count of instances where the model reﬂects on
its predictions, while “correct proportion” denotes the ratio of
accurate reﬂections to the total reﬂections made.
We performed experiments on a dynamic architecture without
the self-reﬂection mechanism. As given in Table III, removing
the self-reﬂective mechanism from the proposed CoLLM leads
to a performance decline. On FD001, the RMSE rises from 12.33
to 12.37 (a 0.3% increase), and the MAE goes from 8.86 to
8.94 (a 0.9% increase). On FD003, the RMSE increases from
11.11 to 11.16 (a 0.45% increase), and the MAE rises from
7.04 to 7.12 (a 1.1% increase). In the self-reﬂection samples,
more than 70% of cases indicate that the SM outperforms
the LM in prediction metrics, which indicates that the LM
is able to correctly self-reﬂect and employ the SM to reﬁne the
prediction results. These ﬁndings demonstrate that the proposed
self-reﬂection mechanism effectively enhances the accuracy of
the dynamic architecture.
In addition, as shown in Fig. 4, when the conﬁdence of the
LM is low, the self-reﬂection mechanism leverages the decision
of the smaller model for correction, mitigating unreliable
predictions in complex scenarios. The upper plot presents

the SM and the LM, illustrating their respective prediction
results. The lower bar chart depicts the difference in prediction
errors between LM and SM [Error(LM - SM)], with red bars
indicating instances where the LM’s error exceeds that of the
SM, and blue bars representing cases where the LM achieves
lower error. A positive difference (red) suggests that the LM
effectively leverages its self-reﬂection mechanism, utilizing the
SM’s predictions to further reﬁne its own results.

V. CONCLUSION
This article proposes CoLLM, a fuzzy collaborative frame-
work that optimizes computational efﬁciency and reliability
for industrial LMs. By constructing a fuzzy decision-making
agent, the framework dynamically selects between LM and
SM based on uncertainty quantiﬁcation of industrial time-series
data, addressing the limitations of static task-level collaboration.
In addition, the self-reﬂection mechanism cross-validates LM
outputs with SM corrections to mitigate catastrophic errors,
improving decision reliability. Experiments demonstrate that
CoLLM reduces computational costs of LMs by 90% while
maintaining accuracy, offering a practical solution for deploy-
ment of industrial LMs.
FutureworkwillexploreintegratingCoLLMwithcloud–edge
collaboration, where LMs are deployed in the cloud and SM at
the edge to better balance performance and latency. We also
aim to develop more precise uncertainty estimation methods to
enhance the accuracy of large–small model collaboration.
